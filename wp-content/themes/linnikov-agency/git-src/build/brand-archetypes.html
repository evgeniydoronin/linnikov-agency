There was a time when the successful creation, construction, marketing, and promotion of brands did not require boundless inspiration or extensive capital. Demand exceeded supply, and markets were not yet saturated.

Traditionally, physical products were distinct from one another, and brands were built upon these differences. This was the state of marketing and commerce for centuries. However, the day came when competition reached a certain level, and all businesses‚Äîwhether it was a multinational cola company or a corner dry cleaner‚Äîencountered a new, complex challenge. Regardless of how efficient a company's production and distribution systems were, or how effectively it cleaned clothes, its methods could be imitated or replicated by competitors.

The world is overloaded with advertising

Nowadays, an average American encounters 6,000 advertisements daily, while a European sees about 4,000. Given that we can barely remember one or two ads from the last day, week, or month, it's apparent that there's a disconnect. Our brains have adapted to cope with the information overload they encounter every second. They don't react to mere facts.

Filtration by the brain

In fact, we remember or consciously register less than 1% of what our eyes see. If we were to quantify the volume of information we encounter in 24 hours, it could fill 176 newspapers. In these circumstances, businesspeople have realized that they have only two main strategic paths for development: either to lower prices or to infuse their products with a unique sense of meaning.

The meaning as a brand asset

BRAND MEANING is the most precious and irreplaceable asset of your brand. The significance your brand holds for people is arguably as important, if not more so, than its functional aspects. This is because meaning resonates on an emotional level, conveying messages like "it feels the same as I do" or "this is for me."

Polar star branding

Branding without a value management system is like sailors trying to find a port in a stormy sea on a starless night. All they need is a reliable and sturdy compass, a well-tuned mechanism that tells them where they are and where they should be heading. Similarly, for a brand manager, the theory of archetypes can serve as such a compass.

You‚Äôre creating products for people, so your target audience should be your primary focus. To understand their values, you need to talk to them:

  • Find out in what situations they use your product.
  • Ask them what feelings they experience when interacting with your product.

These questions will help you understand how your target audience perceives your brand. This translation maintains the intent and clarity of the original text while ensuring it‚Äôs natural in English.

    Innocent

    The Innocent archetype, above all else, wishes to avoid responsibility. They are more inclined to delay making decisions until the last moment, hoping that things will somehow work out on their own. The Innocent aspires to have the perfect job, the perfect spouse, the perfect home, perfect children, and a perfect life. The main promise of the Innocent is that life can be a Paradise.

    This archetype is suitable for your product if it:

    • Offer a simple solution for an existing problem
    • Associated with goodness, morality, nostalgia, childhood
    • Low average price
    • Aim to differentiate from brands with a bad reputation

    Strengths

    Trust and optimism, which endear them to others, thereby gaining assistance and support. Ability to maintain significant independence from the dominant society.

    Challenges

    They are afraid of being punished for something they did badly or wrongly. Boring due to naive innocence.
